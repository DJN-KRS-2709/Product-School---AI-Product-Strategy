## Slide 4 — The Arc

- This is the backbone of the entire course. Six strategic questions, asked in order, each depending on the last:
  - **The Bet** — What should we build, and how do we know fast?
  - **The Moat** — Why won't this get copied in 6 months?
  - **The Margin** — Will this make money or bleed it?
  - **The Contract** — Why will users trust a probabilistic system?
  - **The Guardrails** — What breaks when this scales?
  - **The Pitch** — How do you get this funded, shipped, and adopted?
- The logic: you can't price something you can't defend. You can't govern something users don't trust. Each module builds on the last — that's why it's one program, not six.

## Slide 8 — M1: The Bet

- M1's job: break the old mental model and replace it with probabilistic thinking. Make participants realize their current strategy process doesn't work for AI products.
- Opens with 3 True/False provocations — all FALSE. "The best AI product wins?" "You can predict which feature wins?" "Your discovery process is fast enough?" By #3, the room knows this isn't a beginner course.
- Live diagnosis on a product everyone knows (Notion, Figma, Slack) using the Three-Axis Diagnostic: contextual moat, data advantage, platform exposure. Then they apply it to their own product.
- Peer challenge: partner must lower at least one score. Then name one real AI-native competitor attacking that vulnerability right now.
- Killer memo: "You're the Big Tech exec whose job is to destroy your product. Write a 3-sentence memo." Reveals blind spots self-assessment always misses.
- **Wow moment: build a working prototype with AI tools in 15 minutes.** Not a slide deck about what to build — the thing itself.
- They leave with: Strategy Brief Section 1 + a shareable prototype link. Both carry forward to M2.

---

## Slide 9 — M2 & M3

- **M2 — The Moat:** Focused on defensibility. We teach that data alone isn't a moat — workflow embedding and switching costs are. Participants map their data flywheel loop by loop, score it, find the weakest link. Partner plays Big Tech attacker. The wow moment is The Kill Switch: they design a vendor portability plan to swap models in 48 hours. Most will realize they can't — that's the point.
- **M3 — The Margin:** This is the module nobody else teaches at depth. Most AI courses mention pricing in passing. We spend 2 full hours on it. Participants learn model cascading (cheap model for 80%, frontier for 20%), CPO-level LLMOps levers, and hybrid pricing evolution. They build a real cost curve on their product and stress-test it: what if inference costs 3x? What if their heaviest users double? The wow moment is watching 80% SaaS margins collapse to 40% on their own numbers.
- Case studies: Jasper vs. ChatGPT (the moat that wasn't), Klarna (the Automator that overcorrected).
- **M3 is our highest-differentiation module. The one people will talk about.**

---

## Slide 10 — M4 & M5

- **M4 — The Contract:** Trust is not a function of accuracy — it's a function of perceived control. We teach golden datasets, LLM-as-a-Judge for scaled evals, human-in-the-loop as a product feature (not a crutch), and confidence UX that shows uncertainty instead of hiding it. Participants spec an eval dashboard and write a reliability contract: what they promise users, what they measure, what happens when the system falls below threshold. Wow moment: side-by-side demo proving 85% with transparency beats 95% without.
- **M5 — The Guardrails:** Governance positioned as a sales weapon, not compliance overhead. Companies that can demonstrate auditable AI close enterprise deals faster. We cover agent orchestration boundaries (what can agents decide alone?), org topology for AI, and the shadow AI audit. Wow moment: a 5-minute checklist where they discover unsanctioned AI tools and hidden spend they didn't know existed. Governance stops feeling abstract and starts feeling urgent.
- Both modules cover the strategic 20% a product leader needs — not the operational 80%. They connect to our standalone Evals and Agents courses, but don't replace them.
- If cannibalization question: "Different audience, different depth. This is strategic literacy. Those are operational fluency."

## Slide 11 — M6: The Capstone

- M6 is where everything comes together. The Strategy Brief they've been building for 5 modules gets integrated, refined, and stress-tested.
- First they learn board-ready storytelling: translating AI complexity into investment narratives. Multi-horizon roadmaps (quick wins + bets + moonshots). AI-specific GTM frictions: how to demo probabilistic products, package outcomes not features.
- Then the capstone: 60–75 minutes. Each participant presents their complete Strategy Brief to the room. The room plays the board. "Why should we fund this? What's the risk you haven't addressed? What happens when OpenAI ships this natively?"
- Emotional kicker: they re-read their M1 baseline answer ("What's our AI strategy?") and compare it to the full brief. The distance traveled is the proof the course worked.
- **Energy: hostile but friendly. Intense but supportive.**

## Slide 12 — Design Principles

- 5 design choices that make this best-in-class. The two Carlos should remember:
  - **Build every session** — not just M1. Prototype, cost model, eval dashboard, governance policy, pitch. Hands-on building in every single session.
  - **Opinionated throughout** — every module opens with a bold provocation that challenges conventional wisdom. "Your data is not your moat." "AI will compress your margins before it expands them." Opinions attract senior audiences. Balanced summaries attract beginners.
- Also: consistent case study format across all modules (Bet → Crack → Correction), escalating peer challenge (partner attacks your moat → stress-tests your pricing → red-teams your evals → plays your board), and external-first language (every question is about the product, not the PM).
