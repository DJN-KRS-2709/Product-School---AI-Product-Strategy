# Speaker Notes: AI Product Strategy Certification Pitch

Casual talking points for each slide. Not a script — just the key things to say and the energy to bring.

---

## Slide 1 — Title

**Six Questions. One Strategy Brief. Board-Ready by Friday.**

> "Here's what we've designed. An AI Product Strategy certification for senior PMs who already know AI — they've done AIPC or equivalent — and now need to make the strategic calls: what to build, how to defend it, how to price it, how to govern it, and how to pitch it to their board. Six sessions. One artifact. Let me show you how it works."

- Keep it to 30 seconds. The title does the work.
- Set the tone: this is a proposal with a clear point of view, not a brainstorm.

> "We looked at everything — Reforge, Maven, the top Substack voices, Harvard. Here's what we found."

> "Reforge is great for leadership — how to lead teams in the AI era. But they've fragmented the topic across 6 separate paid courses: AI Strategy, AI Evals, AI Prototyping, AI Growth, AI Productivity, AI Foundations. Six courses, no throughline."

> "Maven has strong tactical courses — build this, ship that. But they stay below the strategy level. No economics module. No governance. No board narrative."

> "AIPC is our own foundation — that's our prerequisite, not our competitor."

> "The gap: nobody owns the strategic middle. How do you build an AI product that's defensible, profitable, and trustworthy? That's our lane."

- If Carlos asks about specific competitors, you have the full competitive analysis in `course-architecture.md`.
- The key insight: Reforge charges for 6 courses to cover what we do in one coherent arc.

## Slide 3 — Positioning

> "Three words. Lead, build, win."

> "Reforge teaches you to lead. Maven teaches you to build. We teach you to win. External focus — building AI products that work in the market."

- Let this slide sit for 10 seconds. The visual does the work — Product School is highlighted, everyone else is grey.

## Slide 4 — The Arc

> "This is the core of the course. Six strategic questions. Each one builds on the last."

> "First — The Bet. What should we build, and how do we know fast?"

> "Then — The Moat. Why won't this get copied in 6 months?"

> "The Margin. Will this make money or bleed it?"

> "The Contract. Why will users trust a probabilistic system?"

> "The Guardrails. What breaks when this scales?"

> "And The Pitch. How do you get this funded, shipped, and adopted?"

> "Every question builds on the answer before it. You can't price something you can't defend. You can't govern something users don't trust. The arc is the product."

- This is the most important slide. Slow down.
- Point to each node as you name it. Let the progression sink in.
- If Carlos is visual, trace the flow: "See how each one depends on the last? That's why it's one program, not six."

## Slide 5 — The Throughline Artifact

> "Here's the design decision that separates us from everyone else. One document. Six layers. Built across all six sessions."

> "It starts as a 1-pager in M1 — just the bet. By M2 they've added the moat analysis. By M3, the cost model. By M4, the eval spec. M5, the governance framework. And by M6, they present the whole thing to the room."

> "Not worksheets stapled together at the end. One living strategy document that grows every session. By M6, it's board-ready."

> "They don't leave with a certificate. They leave with a weapon."

- Hit "weapon" and pause. That's the line that lands.
- If Carlos asks "isn't that just a capstone?" — "No, the capstone is the presentation. The artifact has been building for 5 modules before that."

## Slide 8 — M1: The Bet

> "M1 sets the tone. Three True/False provocations: 'The best AI product wins? FALSE — copycats are everywhere.' 'You can predict which AI feature wins? FALSE — AI produces probability distributions, not guarantees.' 'Your discovery process is fast enough? FALSE — traditional discovery takes 6–8 weeks, AI-powered discovery does it in days.' By the third reveal, the room knows this isn't a beginner course."

> "Then they do a live diagnosis on a product everyone uses — Notion, Figma, Slack. Three-Axis Diagnostic: contextual moat, data advantage, platform exposure. Where does it break?"

> "They apply the same diagnostic to their own product. Their partner's job: challenge the score, not validate it. Then they write a 3-sentence 'killer memo' as the Big Tech exec whose job is to destroy their product."

> "The wow moment: they build a working prototype with v0, Cursor, or Lovable in 15 minutes. They leave with the first section of their Strategy Brief and a shareable link."

- If Carlos asks about the True/False: "It's provocative by design. Senior audiences don't engage with balanced summaries. They engage with bold claims they can argue with. Each module gets its own provocation — we don't front-load all six in M1."

---

## Slide 9 — M2 & M3: The Strategic Core

> "M2 is about survival. Your data isn't your moat — your workflow is. They map their data flywheel, score it, find the weakest link. Then their partner plays the Big Tech attacker trying to kill their product."

> "M3 is the one people will talk about. Nobody else teaches AI economics at this depth. Participants build a cost curve, a cascading model, and a hybrid pricing strategy. They stress-test it: what happens when inference costs 3x?"

> "When we looked at the market, economics was the biggest gap. Everyone mentions pricing. Nobody spends 2 hours on it. We do."

- Hit M3 hardest. This is the module that differentiates the entire course.
- The Klarna case study is perfect here: $40M projected savings, then the CEO admits they overcorrected on cost.

---

## Slide 10 — M4 & M5: Trust & Scale

> "M4 — trust isn't about accuracy. It's about perceived control. 95% accuracy with no audit trail loses to 85% with one. Participants spec an eval dashboard and a reliability contract."

> "M5 — governance isn't compliance overhead. It's a sales weapon. Companies that can demonstrate auditable AI are shortlisting faster. EU AI Act context, Samsung data leak as the cautionary tale."

> "Both modules cover the strategic 20% a product leader needs. Not the operational 80%. They connect to — but don't replace — our dedicated Evals and Agents courses."

- This addresses the overlap question preemptively.
- If Carlos asks about cannibalization: "Different audience, different depth. This is strategic literacy. Those courses are operational fluency."

## Slide 11 — M6: The Capstone

> "M6 is the payoff. Everything from M1–M5 integrates into the full Strategy Brief. Then they present it."

> "The room plays the board. 'Why should we fund this? What's the risk you haven't addressed? What happens when OpenAI ships this natively?'"

> "60 to 75 minutes of capstone. Real challenges. Real pressure. By the end, they've rehearsed the pitch they're going to give on Monday."

- The board simulation is the emotional high point of the course. Make it sound intense but supportive.
- "Hostile but friendly" is the energy.

## Slide 12 — Design Principles

> "Five things that make this best-in-class."

> "First — build every session. Not just M1. Prototype, cost model, eval dashboard, governance policy, pitch. Every session."

> "Second — opinionated throughout. The True/False tone isn't just the opening. Every module has a provocation."

> "Third — consistent case study format. Bet, crack, correction. Klarna, Jasper, Air Canada, Samsung, Duolingo. Same structure, different company."

> "Fourth — peer challenge escalates. Partner attacks your moat, stress-tests your pricing, red-teams your evals. By M6, the whole room is your board."

> "Fifth — external-first language. Every question is about the product, not the PM."

- If Carlos only retains two: #1 (build every session) and #2 (opinionated).
- These are what Reforge and Maven can't replicate.

## General Tips for the Room

- **Carlos is visual.** Don't read the slides. Let them sit. Point to things. The arc flow on slide 4 and the artifact layers on slide 5 are your two most important visuals.
- **Carlos cares about external focus.** He was clear in our March 10 session. Every time you say something, ask yourself: "Is this about the product or about the PM?" It should be about the product.
- **Daria knows the operational detail.** If Carlos asks about logistics, exercises, or templates — she can speak to that. You focus on the strategic arc and the judgment calls.
- **Don't compare to Vibe Coding.** It's a different course, different audience. The model (Dejan leads, Daria supports, GitHub repo, Tuesday/Friday cadence) is the same. The content is independent.
- **If the conversation goes deep on one module,** redirect: "The detail is in the course architecture doc. Today I want alignment on the arc and the six questions. If we're aligned, the module detail follows."
