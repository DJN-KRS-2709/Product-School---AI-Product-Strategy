# Module 6 — The Pitch
## Speaker Briefing for Carlos

### What this module is about

This is the capstone. Five components built across five sessions — now they assemble one story and pitch it under fire. The emotional arc is performance anxiety turning into confidence. They walk in nervous about presenting something they've been building in fragments; they leave having said it out loud to peers playing their board. That's the transformation: from worksheets to narrative.

The traditional assumption this module breaks: "A roadmap is a strategy." It's not — it's a delivery plan. A strategy answers why this bet, why now, and why us. The roadmap follows after the story is clear. Stripe's model is the reference: public writing plus principles → bets → then epics. Roadmap follows.

They leave with four artifacts: a finalized living strategy dashboard (all five components visualized), a multi-horizon roadmap, a board pitch they've rehearsed out loud, and a before/after comparison of their M1 baseline to prove their own growth.

### The flow and why it's structured this way

The first half is a fast lecture block — provocation, multi-horizon roadmaps, metrics, board questions, GTM frictions, Duolingo case. We cover a lot of ground quickly because the real work starts after the break. The break sits before the heaviest applied stretch in the entire course: finalize the living strategy, build the roadmap, run the board simulation. Over a third of the session is on the capstone — rehearsal, not consumption.

### Slide 1 — Title

Capstone energy: five components into one story, and they pitch it with the room playing their board. Stakes and clarity beat another framework slide. The wow: "In about 2 hours you'll have said this out loud to peers playing your board."

### Slide 2 — Agenda

More than a third of the time is on the capstone. Fast lecture block first: Duolingo case, moat card, then work plus simulation. The agenda reassures people that the heavy lifting is collaborative, not solo.

### Slide 3 — The Full Arc

Closure — they built a system, not slide decks. Three bundles (Components 1–2, 3–4, 5) keep the wall of text off the screen. The Adobe Firefly example walks through the arc: bet, workflow moat, bundle margin, IP plus opt-in trust contract — all in about 30 seconds if there's time. This is the first moment where the whole course clicks as one connected argument.

### Slide 4 — Provocation

Roadmap-as-strategy is the number one anti-pattern for PMs under OKR pressure. Poll each FALSE claim — thumbs, stand/sit, or Zoom reactions. The Stripe angle: public writing plus principles → bets → then epics; roadmap follows. Partner flash: which claim did they believe? Twenty seconds — surfaces assumptions they're still carrying.

### Slide 5 — Multi-Horizon Roadmaps

Boards fund portfolios across time, not one backlog. Shopify-style story: H1 core polish, H2 AI in workflow (like Sidekick), H3 platform ambition — same narrative spine. "Point to H1 / H2 / H3" — where is their real weight? Most rooms cluster at H1, which is the honest answer but also the vulnerability. A board wants to see all three horizons funded with different risk profiles.

### Slide 6 — AI Metrics

Without AI-quality plus cost signals, execs default to "black box plus liability." Microsoft Copilot and Datadog flavor: pair the usage story with guardrails plus degradation visibility. Gokul's hook: durability matters more than vanity growth; retention is the quality signal. Quick poll: "Number one AI metric you'd put on a board slide tomorrow?" Go round-robin — this surfaces how few people have a clear answer.

### Slide 7 — Four Board Questions (3 Buckets)

Executive working memory operates in buckets, not your stack trace. Notion example: AI as workflow wedge plus distribution, not model trivia first. Stripe example: money movement plus risk, not "LLM inside." Sixty-second pair practice: case (bet + moat), model (margin), risks (contract + guardrails). This is the dress rehearsal structure for the board simulation later.

### Slide 8 — AI GTM Frictions

This primes the objections they'll face in the capstone. Salesforce and Intercom-style patterns: scripted demos, governance packs, containment metrics. Pair exercise: which friction is scariest for their buyer? Forty-five seconds each. Naming the friction now means they can preempt it in their pitch.

### Slide 9 — Case: Duolingo

Narrative repair under scrutiny — same muscle as board Q&A. Bet → crack ("does it teach?") → fix (outcomes over tech). Poll or hand-raise: is their story mostly tech or mostly outcomes? Most people skew tech, which is exactly the mistake Duolingo made before correcting. The lesson: lead with what changed for the user, not what model you used.

### Slide 10 — Moat Scorecard

Gives vocabulary and self-defense for the inevitable question: "Why can't someone copy this?" Snowflake vs. Nvidia: different moat shapes, same scorecard. Two minutes solo scores → two minutes partner compare: where did you disagree? The disagreement forces evidence, not opinion.

### Slide 11 — Break

Reset before integrate plus simulation. Tease what's coming: back → 15 minutes to finalize, 10 on roadmap, 35 on the board simulation.

### Slide 12 — Finalize Living Strategy

Integration skill matters more than starting over — panic rewrite wastes the cohort's work. Three rows only on slide; details live in their docs. Optional: 90-second elevator to partner, then silent work.

The Pitch Assembler tool is linked on-slide — it pulls all five strategy components from their previous tools into one pitch outline. If they saved their work in M1–M5, most of the fields are already there. Fill gaps, export, and that's their board deck skeleton.

This is the dashboard moment. Have everyone open the Living Strategy Builder and click "Pull from M1" through "Pull from M5" — their vulnerability bars, flywheel radar, cost curve with scenario sliders, trust meter, and maturity spider chart all render live. The strategy health score at the top gives them a composite number. Then hit "Board View" — the form fields disappear and what's left is a clean, visual dashboard with just the charts and key numbers. That's their board artifact. Not a 20-page document. Not a slide deck. A living dashboard they built across six sessions. If they want a text export, the "Copy Board Summary" button produces a concise executive format they can paste into an email or Slack.

### Slide 13 — AI Bet Evaluator

This is the meta-move — using AI to evaluate their own AI strategy. The prompt is structured around the same five dimensions they've been learning all course: bet validation, defensibility, pricing alignment, trust/reliability, and governance. This creates a reusable workflow they take back to work.

**Mental model:** The evaluation dimensions aren't a fixed rubric — they're the same lenses from M1–M5 turned into a systematic AI query. After the course, they can adapt the dimensions for any new bet: add domain-specific criteria, change the scoring scale, or focus on the 2–3 dimensions most relevant to their current decision.

How it works: participants open the AI Bet Evaluator tool, paste their strategy components, select which dimensions to evaluate, and generate a structured prompt. They paste the prompt into ChatGPT or Claude and get a scored, specific critique. Give them about 5 minutes to generate and run the prompt, about 3 minutes to review the AI's output. The critique surfaces gaps they should address before the board simulation. If time is tight, this can be done after the session — the tool and prompt are permanent.

The AI Bet Evaluator tool is linked on-slide — paste your strategy, pick evaluation lenses, get a structured prompt you can run in any AI tool.

### Slide 14 — Build the Roadmap

Roadmap proves strategy moves through time, not a wishlist. Amazon/AWS pattern: near-term cash and ops fund platform bets. Force an H3 line. Stripe-style check: every H2 bet has a kill line or falsifiable signal. If someone's roadmap is all H1, push them: "Where's the bet that scares you? That's H3."

### Slide 15 — Board Simulation

Single best bang-for-minute in the whole certification. Mix company size plus archetype; volunteers first. If the room is quiet, seed Stripe-style dollar plus risk questions. The debrief insight: decks that landed led with the thesis first, tech stack second. Nobody on a board cares about your model architecture — they care about the bet, the moat, and the margin.

### Slide 16 — M1 → Now

Emotional proof of growth without forcing vulnerability. Same CEO prompt as Module 1; the delta is the progress. Keep sharing optional. Most people will be genuinely surprised by how different their answer is now compared to their M1 baseline. That gap is the course working.

### Slide 17 — What You Leave With

Artifact over attendance — name the components once, let pride land. Figma/Notion energy: a toolkit they can reuse Monday. The living strategy is the headline artifact. It's a dashboard, not a document — interactive, visual, continuously updated. Each module fed one component. Others at their company can open it, add to it, and it doesn't require an annual rewrite. The tools and all the data persist in the browser. They can export the full strategy as markdown anytime, share the board summary, or keep building on it as their product evolves.

Formal close after the emotional peak of slides 15–16. Done equals 6 modules, a living strategy, and capstone delivered.
