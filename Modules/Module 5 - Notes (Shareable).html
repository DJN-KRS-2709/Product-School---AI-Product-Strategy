00 · What this module is about

From do they trust it? to what happens when this scales?

Module 4 was about product-level trust — golden datasets, confidence UX, reliability contracts, the promises one product makes to its users. Module 5 zooms out. We ask a harder pair of questions: does this product get smarter the more it's used, and what already broke that nobody told you about?

The shift is from craft to exposure. M4 was: can you build something trustworthy? M5 is: what's already happening that you don't know about? Agents picking actions on their own. Employees pasting confidential data into tools nobody approved. Feedback loops that exist on paper and trash every signal in practice. Most experienced PMs are surprised by how much of this is already running inside their own org.

The assumption to break early: "Compliance is a one-time checklist." In AI, systems either compound or degrade silently. There's no steady state. Traditional products don't get worse when you're not looking. AI products do.

You leave with one artifact, 05-the-guardrails/compounding-system.md, holding three blocks of work: a Compounding Sketch (Feedback Loops + Context Connectivity sections — three loops that either learn, scale, or fail), a Governance One-Pager (Governance Policy + Agent Topology sections — autonomy boundaries, escalation triggers, audit cadence, regulatory exposure), and a Shadow AI Audit Starter (the Shadow AI Audit section — five workarounds your users have built around your product with AI, classified by signal, with a build / partner / ignore decision on each). Backed by three interactive tools — the Compounding System Designer, the Governance Policy Drafter, and the Shadow AI Audit — that do the structural lift.

02 · Provocation and the compounding concept

Governance is a sales asset. Most products only scale.

Slide 5Provocation

The framing for the whole session, with three statements that sound like industry common sense and turn out to be wrong:

  1. "Governance slows us down." False. Salesforce built what they call the Einstein Trust Layer and they literally sell it as a product feature. Enterprise buyers ask for it by name. Governance isn't slowing them down — it's their GTM strategy. They turned a compliance cost into a revenue driver.
  2. "We can manage AI tools organically." False. Spotify woke up one day with around a thousand AI tools floating across the organization. Different teams, different vendors, no coordination. They consolidated down to fifteen. Organic management is chaos with a nicer name.
  3. "Agents are just automation with better UX." False. Agents choose actions and chain tools — fundamentally a different category. Air Canada's chatbot invented a refund policy that didn't exist, a grieving customer relied on it, and a tribunal held the company liable for what the bot said. The bot speaks for the brand.

The point isn't pessimism. It's that the same beliefs that keep classic SaaS humming actively misroute AI strategy. The rest of the module gives you the tools to push back against each one — first with compounding systems, then with governance, then with a shadow AI audit you can actually hand to someone Monday morning.

Slide 6Compounding Systems

The conceptual foundation of the first half, and arguably the most strategically important concept in the module. The reframe: there is a difference between scales and compounds, and most AI products are doing the first and calling it the second.

Scales
More volume
↑ volume · flat quality · linear cost. Fine, normal — but a commodity with variable costs.
Compounds
Each touch → smarter
Every interaction makes the next one better. Cost per win falls. A different economic animal.

Three mechanisms make compounding actually happen: recursive learning (outputs and corrections feed the model), cross-domain transfer (a win in product A lifts product B next door), and network intelligence (fleet-level patterns, privacy-gated). Two anchors:

  • Duolingo — every learner interaction sharpens lesson difficulty for the next learner. Your struggle with Spanish past tense makes the next person's lesson better. Recursive learning at the lesson level.
  • ServiceTitan — 32 products on one vertical stack, each feeding context to the others. HVAC scheduling data makes the invoicing smarter, which makes dispatching smarter, which feeds back into scheduling. Cross-domain transfer at the stack level.

Why care: most AI products scale without compounding, and that means they're commodities with variable costs. If your system doesn't get smarter with use, someone else's will — and they'll eat your lunch while you're still arguing about the next feature.

03 · Design Your Compounding System

One forcing function: freeze three months.

Slide 7Governance & Compounding Lab

Section break introducing the first build block. Three exercises across the next stretch of the session: design your compounding system (now), draft a governance policy (after the lecture stack), and audit shadow AI (after the peer challenge). Forty minutes total. Everything you draft goes straight into 05-the-guardrails/compounding-system.md.

Slide 8Design Your Compounding System

Ten minutes of solo work. The slide shows a Feedback Loops table that mirrors compounding-system.md exactly. Three pre-labeled rows — Recursive Learning, Cross-Domain Transfer, Network Intelligence — but feel free to rename, add, or drop rows. Your product might have two loops; it might have six.

For each loop, four short fields: the input (what data goes in), the output (what improves), compounds Y/N (is it a flywheel or just a feature?), and status — active if it's shipping, broken if it exists but doesn't learn, missing if the loop just isn't there. Be honest about status. Most rows for most products are missing, and that is the finding.

Two small footer fields go below the table. Broken loop (partner found) stays blank for now — your partner identifies that during the peer challenge a few slides from here. Context Connectivity — one line on where knowledge silos in your org. If your support team's corrections never reach the eng team's models, that's a context-connectivity break, and it's almost always the answer to "why doesn't our product actually learn from real usage?"

Freeze your product for three months — about one frontier cycle. No updates, no improvements. Still winning? If yes, you're not compounding. You're just scaling.

That's the forcing function. Twelve months used to be the cliché version; in AI, three is more honest — three months is roughly one model cycle, and it cleanly separates products with real compounding loops from products that ride model upgrades pretending to be flywheels.

The Compounding System Designer tool linked on the slide pre-loads the three rows, gives you a live diagnostic per row, runs the freeze-test logic, and copies your output as Markdown straight into the repo. Good answers name specific data flows — something like "support ticket corrections feed the fine-tuning pipeline weekly" — not aspirations.

The bar for today: three loops on the page with honest status labels. One loop that's genuinely active beats three that are aspirational, every time.

04 · The governance half — ladder, agents, ROI, Samsung

Compliance → trust architecture → governance as GTM.

Slide 9Responsible AI Maturity

A three-level ladder that reframes how most teams think about compliance.

  • Level 1 — Compliance. EU AI Act, GDPR, CCPA. The minimum bar. Necessary, table stakes, doesn't sell anything.
  • Level 2 — Trust Architecture. What you built in M4 — evals, confidence UX, reliability contracts. You're ahead of most companies if you're here.
  • Level 3 — Governance = GTM. Model cards, bias posture, your trust infrastructure as a selling point. Salesforce's Einstein Trust Layer is the textbook case — governance is in the actual sales pitch, and "show me your AI governance framework" is becoming a standard procurement question.

Most orgs plateau at Level 1 or 2 and completely miss the revenue opportunity. The counter-anchor: Google Gemini's image generation incident — paused publicly for remediation in front of the entire market. A velocity-vs-safety decision made on stage, in real time. The kind of moment that makes Level 1 feel inadequate.

Slide 10Agent Governance

Multi-agent systems are fundamentally different from chatbots. Authorization, memory, liability, tool-calling in series — a different animal. Four governance knobs that survive model churn:

KnobWhat it controlsConcrete move
Autonomy Draft ≠ send. Read ≠ write. Best practice: draft anything, send nothing without approval — until eval data proves >99% on the action.
Tool Calls Every call is a risk surface. Whitelist the allowed tools. Rate-limit them (10 refunds/hour). Log every call with reasoning + result.
Memory What persists, who reads it. Three explicit classes: short-term (session-only), long-term (per-user), shared (across users — almost always a hard no for customer-facing).
Chain When B fails on A's output, who owns the handoff? Named ownership per handoff. A "stop the chain" trigger if any agent in the line fails its eval. Don't let chains run silently.

The real-world anchor is Microsoft Copilot with Graph and plugins. They log and whitelist like any production system, and they tier autonomy explicitly — read your calendar (auto), draft an email (auto), send the email (one-click human approval). The principle that survives the next model swap: design around principles, not model names. Specific models churn every six months. Your governance framework needs to outlast that churn.

Slide 11Agentic vs. Traditional ROI

Your execs are going to compare agent investments to traditional Robotic Process Automation (RPA — UiPath, Automation Anywhere, the "bots that click on screens" category that runs in most large enterprises). Same budget line, same quarterly review, completely different economics. If you don't get ahead of that conversation, you lose it.

Traditional RPA
Fixed paths
Linear ROI. Snaps on edge cases. Predictable to budget, brittle in practice.
Agentic
Variable, learning
Token costs swing. Quality is fuzzy. Eats exceptions — if governed. Different P&L, different audit trail.

Three decision levers to use with finance: complexity (simple and predictable → scripts; messy and edge-case-heavy → agents earn their keep), error cost (low tolerance for mistakes means the human review tax kills agent ROI fast), and learning (agents only pay off if the feedback loops from Slide 6 actually ship — not "we plan to ship them," actually ship them). Same slide as RPA on a spectrum, very different business case at the bottom.

Slide 12Samsung Case

The case study most students remember six months later. Samsung employees started using ChatGPT for code review, meeting notes, and process optimization. Totally understandable — the tools were amazing, people wanted to use them, nobody had told them not to. Within roughly a month, three data leaks: source code, meeting content, and fabrication data, all entering vendor training pipelines. No AI use policy. No audit trail. Nobody even knew it was happening until it was too late.

Act One
The Bet
ChatGPT adopted for code, notes, meetings. Fast wins, no guardrails, broad enthusiasm.
Act Two
The Crack
3 leaks in ~1 month — source · meetings · fab data → vendor pipeline. No policy, no audit trail, panic.
Act Three
The Correction
Ban all external AI tools. Rebuild internally from scratch. Massive productivity hit.

The lesson isn't "don't use AI tools." It's that the absence of governance isn't a compliance gap — it's a strategy failure. The ban cost Samsung more than the leaks did.

The reflex reaction most students have is "this couldn't happen at my company." In every cohort that bet has been wrong. It's already happening. Most of it is your CISO's problem to manage — employees pasting work into vendor models is real but it's not, structurally, a PM decision. The version of shadow AI you can act on is different, and it's the next chapter.

Slide 13The Shadow AI Audit (framework) — for PMs

This is the reframe. Samsung-style shadow AI is your CISO's problem. The version you can act on as a product manager is what your users are building with AI on top of, alongside, or to replace pieces of your product. Same name, different problem, very different opportunity — because every user-side workaround is either a feature request you weren't reading as one, or a capability someone else is going to ship before you do.

The four-step framework — same shape, redirected at the user-side: Discover → Signal → Prioritize → Decide.

  1. Discover — where users reach for AI you didn't ship. Six places to look, none of them surveys: support tickets (search for "ChatGPT," "Claude," "Zapier"), user interviews ("walk me through your workflow"), public forums and Reddit, Zapier/Make integration directories, API usage patterns (big exports without writes-back = enrichment elsewhere), and social media (power users tweet their workflows).
  2. Signal — what does each workaround tell you? Four signal types, and you'll use them to prioritize: workflow gap (they're chaining your output into another step), trust gap (they're double-checking your output with another model — that one stings), capability gap (they want something you don't do), pricing gap (they're getting it externally because it's cheaper or metered differently).
  3. Prioritize — frequency × strategic relevance. A workaround mentioned once is a curiosity. One you see in fifteen tickets, six interviews, and four Zapier recipes is a feature you should already be building.
  4. Decide — three options. Build (absorb the capability natively), Partner (strike an official integration with the external tool), or Ignore (accept some users will keep doing it externally — it's not strategic for you to own). Ignore is a legitimate answer. The trap is calling everything "build" because building feels like ambition.

This is the best discovery research nobody runs. Your users are already telling you what's missing — they just don't open a ticket called "feature request." They paste your output into ChatGPT and post about it. Read what they post.

05 · Draft Your Governance Policy

One page. CISO-readable. Worked example included.

Slide 14Draft Your Governance Policy

Ten minutes. The slide layout mirrors compounding-system.md → Governance Policy exactly — five fields plus an optional Agent Topology strip. The brief is: one page your CISO wouldn't redline on sight.

  • Scope — what this policy covers. Be specific. "All customer-facing AI features in our SMB product line" beats "AI in our company."
  • Autonomy boundaries — what agents can do solo vs. what needs a human. From Slide 10: which tool calls are draft-only, which auto-execute.
  • Escalation triggers — when humans must enter. Common triggers: confidence below threshold, novel customer-facing content, anything legal / financial / medical, anything irreversible.
  • Audit cadence — real-time / daily / weekly / monthly / quarterly. Pick one and name the owner.
  • Regulatory exposure — EU AI Act risk tier (minimal / limited / high), plus GDPR, HIPAA, or sector-specific regimes that apply.

The worked example used through the rest of the course — the customer support copilot. The slide tiles are clickable; each one reveals the example for that field. Combined, it looks like this:

FieldSupportCopilot v1.2
Scope AI features in the SupportCopilot product line — automated reply drafting, routing, and resolution scoring. Excludes internal-only analytics dashboards (separate data-team policy).
Autonomy Drafting replies — auto. Sending replies under $0 risk (FAQ, account info) — auto. Sending replies with promised remedies (refunds, credits, escalations) — human approval required, even if AI confidence > 95%. Closing tickets without reply — never auto.
Escalation (1) Confidence < 70%. (2) Customer message flagged legal / medical / distressed. (3) Any reply mentioning refund, credit, or policy exception. (4) Customer requests human. (5) ≥ 3 turns in one conversation.
Audit cadence Weekly — automated eval vs. golden dataset (PM: Sam). Monthly — human review of 50 random conversations + all escalations (Legal: Priya). Quarterly — full policy review with security + legal (CTO sign-off).
Regulatory EU AI Act — limited risk (customer service is non-prohibited). GDPR — applies; data minimization in prompts, no training on customer PII. SOC 2 controls in place for log retention.

See how specific that is? You could hand it to legal, ops, or a buyer and they could actually evaluate it. Compare to "we have an AI governance policy" — which means nothing. The act of writing the policy isn't the document. It's the forced specificity. Writing it makes you decide what you actually allow.

Below the five fields is a small Agent Topology strip — only fill it in if your product ships agents. Two short notes per agent: what it can do, and what it can't.

Open the Governance Policy Drafter tool from the slide. Same five fields, a "load worked example" button that drops in the SupportCopilot policy as a starter, structured rows for autonomy decisions and audit cadence, progress tracking, and a "copy as Markdown" button that exports straight into your repo. Use it or write directly into the file — your call.

One honest framing for the room: think of this as starter vocabulary for conversations with legal, security, and compliance. You'll map it onto your org's existing policies and committees, not replace them. Nobody loves it when product shows up saying "we wrote you a new policy, you're welcome." Don't make enemies of the people you need.

Slide 17Peer Challenge: "What Feedback Loop Is Broken?"

Twelve minutes total, six per direction. Each partner audits the other's compounding-system.md entry through two lenses:

  • Loops. Is there a broken handoff? Theoretical learning that never actually happens? Corrections getting trashed instead of feeding back into the model? An active row that doesn't survive a five-minute interrogation?
  • Governance. Unapproved agent action? Missing data class? A Samsung-style paste path where sensitive data could leak? An Air Canada-style customer bot with no guardrails?

The repo capture is two short fields: Broken loop identified by partner and Fix plan. One specific broken loop, one specific fix. The pattern to enforce on yourself and your partner: named flow → concrete failure → measurable consequence. "Could improve data classification" is a feeling. "Support bot sends full conversation transcripts to the vendor with no PII stripping" is a finding.

If your partner only found nice things to say, you need a tougher partner.

The slide title is about loops, but the on-screen boxes show both Loops and Governance for a reason: your partner is auditing the whole guardrails entry. The formal repo capture is the loop-finding (it's the only "partner found" field the repo exposes), but if a partner surfaces a great governance gap, fold it back into your own Autonomy or Escalation fields on the spot. Don't let governance findings die just because there's no dedicated field.

Slide 18Shadow AI Audit (Lab Intro)

Section break introducing the best discovery research nobody runs. This isn't an IT audit; it's a roadmap audit. You're about to surface what your users are doing with AI around your product — the workarounds, the integrations, the "I had to use ChatGPT to do X" patterns — and decide whether to build, partner, or ignore each one.

Slide 19Build — Shadow AI Audit (user-side)

Ten minutes. The slide shows the Shadow AI Audit table — three rows by four columns: Workaround / Signal / Frequency / Decision. That maps onto the repo template's four columns: Tool = workaround, Owner = signal source (support, interview, Zapier directory, etc.), Risk H/M/L = frequency, Decision = build / partner / ignore (the repo placeholder still says keep/govern/kill — overwrite it). Three rows is the floor; aim for five if you've got time.

Where does the signal come from if you don't already have a list in your head? Your support team has it. Your CS team has it. Your developer-relations folks have it. Search your support tool for "ChatGPT," "Claude," "Zapier," and your top competitor's name. Five mentions and you've got a pattern.

Below the table, three summary stats: Workarounds found · Build candidates · Adjacent spend. Adjacent spend is the one that turns heads in a roadmap review — if your users are collectively paying $X per month for external AI to do things adjacent to your product, that's the budget that becomes available the moment you ship the native version. In one recent cohort, a PM calculated her users were paying ~$14/month each on top of her product just to use ChatGPT for report summarization. The native feature shipped six weeks later.

Be ruthless with the Risk tiers. "Medium" is the lazy answer — push to High or Low. The Decision column has three real options: keep (safe and useful), govern (useful but needs guardrails), or kill (the data path is unsafe and there's no fix). Pick one villain tool right now — the one that made your stomach drop during the Samsung story.

The Shadow AI Audit tool from the slide walks you through discovery, risk-scoring, and triage with exportable results. Export the results when you're done — this is the artifact you're handing to someone on Monday, and being able to send "here's our shadow AI inventory, scored, with decisions" in a single message is the whole point of the exercise.

Slide 22Key Takeaways

Takeaway 1
Compounding beats scaling.

Most AI products scale without getting smarter. If your system doesn't compound — corrections feeding training, cross-domain transfer, network intelligence — it's a commodity with variable costs. The freeze-test (three months, no updates, still winning?) is the cheap forcing function that separates real compounding from "we're just riding model upgrades."

Takeaway 2
Governance done well is a GTM asset.

Most orgs plateau at Level 1 (compliance) and quietly miss the revenue opportunity. Enterprise buyers increasingly ask "show me your governance" in procurement — Salesforce sells the Einstein Trust Layer as part of the product, not as a footnote. The jump from "we comply" to "we can prove how this works" closes deals faster.

Takeaway 3
Shadow AI is a strategy signal, not a compliance gap.

Samsung's three-act tragedy is what shadow AI looks like inside the org — and that's your CISO's job to manage, not yours. The version you can act on is what users are building with AI around your product. Every workaround is either a feature request you weren't reading as one, or a capability someone else will ship before you do. The audit isn't about catching anyone. It's about finding where your product is not good enough yet and what your users are already paying someone else to fill.

The single line to hold onto, if you only hold onto one: systems that compound get the upside; systems that govern keep it; systems that audit see what's actually there.
