Provocation · 8 Minutes

AI Will Compress Your Margins
Before It Expands Them.

Vote first. Then we reveal. Then we argue.
NOT YET
"AI will lower our costs."
Eventually — but not yet. Inference costs are real and variable. The average AI product sees gross margins drop from 80% (SaaS) to 40–60%. Most leaders don't see this until the P&L lands.
FALSE
"We should price per seat, like we always have."
Seat-based pricing works for access products. AI delivers work products — tasks completed, outcomes achieved. Pricing seats for a system that does the work is leaving money on the table.
FALSE
"Scale will fix the economics."
Scale is dead as a moat for pure software (Gokul Rajaram). Scaling a money-losing AI product just scales the losses. The fix is architectural and strategic, not volumetric.

Lecture · Point 3 of 4

Two Pricing Worlds

Access products vs. Work products — fundamentally different economics.
Access Product
Price per seat
The product is a tool. Humans use it. Figma, Notion, Slack. Seat-based pricing makes sense — you're selling access to capability.
Work Product
Price per outcome
The product does the work. AI completes tasks. Klarna (per resolution), Harvey (per brief). Price the result, not the access.

The labor TAM: AI is shifting from SaaS to services. If your product replaces $150K/year in human labor, pricing at $50/seat/month is a 99% discount on the value you deliver.

Lecture · Point 4 of 4

Commoditize the Complement

Where does the profit pool live — data or workflow?
Strategy A
Own the Data
Proprietary data is the moat. Workflow tools on top can be swapped. Profit pool: data licensing, training, insights.
Strategy B
Own the Workflow
Workflow embedding is the moat. Use best available models. Profit pool: process automation, outcome delivery.

Gokul: Price increases beat cost decreases for improving margins. Don't just cut inference costs — charge more by demonstrating more value.

Square model: Some products exist for profit, some for retention. Not every AI feature needs to be a profit center.

Case Study · 7 Minutes

The Automator That Overcorrected

The Bet
Klarna deployed AI to handle customer support. Handled 2/3 of all chats in month one — equivalent to 700 full-time agents. Thesis: automate support, cut costs.
The Crack
Inference costs scaled with volume. Complex cases still needed humans. Variable AI costs replaced fixed human costs — P&L became less predictable, not more.
The Correction
Moved to cascading — simple queries to cheap models, disputes to human + AI copilot. Shifted from cost-cutting to value narrative: speed, 24/7, multilingual.

Strategic question for the room:

Are you pricing for the headline or the P&L?

Peer Challenge · 13 Minutes

"What If Inference Costs 3x?"

Stress Test 2: Usage Explosion
Heaviest segment doubles usage. Power users now cost 5x what they pay. How do you adjust pricing without losing them?

After both rounds: write down the single biggest economic vulnerability in your model.
