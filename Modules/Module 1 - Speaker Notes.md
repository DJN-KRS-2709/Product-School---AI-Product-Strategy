# Module 1 — The Bet

## Slide 1 — Title
- **Why:** Set energy — working session, not passive lecture; name the shift from deterministic planning to AI-era bets.
- Hook: they leave with Component 1 + a prototype; same strategic questions whether they're bolting on AI or AI-native.
- Optional: "Point to Diagnose / Discover / Decide — which feels hardest for you right now?" (quick read of the room)

## Slide 2 — Why AI Strategy Is Different
- **Why:** This is the opening thesis for the entire course. Traditional product strategy makes five assumptions that AI breaks: deterministic outputs, fixed costs, clear competitive boundaries, trust by default, and annual planning cycles. Name all five explicitly — this table sets up every module that follows.
- Walk through the five rows. Don't lecture — let the visual do the work. ~90 seconds.
- Close with: "Which shift have you felt most in your product?" Quick show of hands or point-to-answer. This reads the room and tells you which modules will hit hardest for this cohort.
- Each row maps to a module: probabilistic outputs → M1, platform encroachment → M2, variable costs → M3, trust → M4, planning cadence → M5. Don't name the mapping — let them discover it over six sessions.
- This slide is the one they'll reference when someone at work asks "why can't we just use our normal strategy process?"

## Slide 3 — Agenda
- **Why:** Reduce anxiety, show it's applied-heavy — they'll *do* the arc, not memorize it.
- 2 hours: provocation → short theory → case → framework → break → diagnose → pair → build → synthesize.
- Don't read every line; hit "three exercises, one case, one prototype."

## Slide 4 — Opening Question
- **Why:** Instructor + room see who's in the building; both paths (A vs B) share the same frameworks.
- Show of hands or "point A or B" — under 2 min.
- Ground rule: real product at their company, not a startup fantasy.

## Slide 5 — Baseline Write
- **Why:** Private baseline for M6 comparison; surfaces how fuzzy "AI strategy" language is before tools.
- 3 min silent — CEO question, 3 sentences, no polish.
- Don't debrief yet; "you'll cringe at this in a good way by M6."

## Slide 6 — True/False Provocation
- **Why:** Uncomfortable truths stick; primes moats (M2), discovery speed, non-determinism.
- Vote each (hands, Zoom reaction, or poll) *before* reveal — tension is the point.
- FALSE / why (short): (1) Best product ≠ win if platforms copy you — moats matter. (2) Outcomes are distributions; big wins often = new behaviors. (3) 6–8 week discovery vs days with AI-assisted loops — competitors won't wait.

## Slide 7 — The Arc
- **Why:** Orient on the six-question spine; today = only "The Bet."
- Trace M1→M6 in ~90 sec; no deep M3–M5 spoilers.
- "Living strategy" = cumulative artifact, not one-off worksheets.

## Slide 8 — Annual Cycles Are Dead
- **Why:** Permission to stop pretending Q3 commitments map to model curves; Cisco quote = external voice, not ours.
- Paraphrase Jeetu Patel — variability + probabilistic models, not "bad execution."
- **Wow:** Ask "Who still has a frozen annual roadmap for AI bets?" — show of hands.

## Slide 9 — Discovery Is Too Slow
- **Why:** Concrete proof (Notion) beats abstract "AI is faster."
- Notion: ~6-week loop → ~4 days on AI editor insights (clustering / synthesis narrative).
- Name-check **Intercom / Sprig-class** stacks on slide as "how teams actually compress research."
- **Poll:** "Your discovery cycle — under 2 wks / 2–6 wks / 6+ wks?" Point or raise hands.

## Slide 10 — Probabilistic Thinking
- **Why:** Core M1 reframe — leaders are trained to promise outcomes; AI rewards bet + kill criteria + learning cadence.
- Old vs new cards on slide = anchor; Bard→Gemini = narrative pivot when the product is inherently variable.
- Non-consumption: Shopify / Gamma / Granola — behaviors that didn't exist; TAM looks "small" until it doesn't.
- Optional aside: pattern-matching misses (e.g. Quinc story) if time.
- **Pair — 30 sec:** "What's one outcome your leadership still asks you to 'guarantee'?"

## Slide 11 — Case Study: Notion
- **Why:** Same industry as them — docs / knowledge — so it feels transferable.
- Bet → Crack → Correction fast; emphasize *time* as the weapon.
- **Discussion:** "Which pace matches you — Notion-speed or research-first?" 2–3 voices, then move.

## Slide 12 — 5 AI Value Archetypes
- **Why:** Archetype drives margin, governance, and what "good" looks like — teams skip this then wonder why economics don't work.
- **Mental model framing:** These five archetypes are a thinking tool, not a taxonomy. Most products blend 2–3. The point is knowing which archetype's *economics and trust profile* dominates your product, because that shapes everything from pricing (M3) to eval design (M4). Push participants to identify their primary + secondary, and name what doesn't fit.
- Examples on slide: UiPath, GitHub Copilot, Palantir, Midjourney, Zapier AI — walk 1 line each.
- **Quick poll:** "Which archetype is your product?" — hands or chat; no debate, just self-label.

## Slide 13 — Break
- **Why:** Reset before 25+ min of applied work; laptops open after.
- 5 min; "honesty + laptop" on return.

## Slide 14 — Three-Axis Diagnostic
- **Why:** Shared vocabulary for moat / data / platform risk before they score themselves.
- **Mental model framing:** The three axes are a lens, not a final score. The numbers force honesty — "all 5s" means you haven't looked hard enough. But the axes are deliberately broad: participants should adapt the scoring criteria to their specific domain. A healthcare AI's "platform risk" looks very different from a consumer app's.
- Name the three axes; 1 = pain, 5 = strong — partner will challenge later.
- **Calibrate aloud** using on-slide cos: Figma · Slack · thin ChatGPT UI · Zapier — one axis each where they win or get eaten.

## Slide 15 — Live Diagnosis
- **Why:** Demystify the rubric on a product everyone knows; argument = learning.
- Pick one: Notion, Figma, Linear, Slack — "Apple / Google / OpenAI ships X native — then what?"
- Calibrate scores with the room; messy is good.

## Slide 16 — Your Diagnostic
- **Why:** Abstract framework → their product; scorecard is what M2 pressure-tests.
- 10 min individual — scores + one vulnerability written down.
- Ruthless honesty; all 5s = red flag. Roam — they should be writing.
- **Tool:** "Open the Vulnerability Scorecard — it auto-calculates your composite score and highlights your weakest axis. When you're done, hit Export — that markdown travels to M2."

## Slide 17 — Pair Challenge
- **Why:** Social proof + discomfort beat solo rationalization; surfaces real competitors.
- 5 min each: partner *lowers* at least one score; name a real attacker on that axis today.
- **Prompt:** 60 sec solo before naming the competitor — specificity matters.

## Slide 18 — In the Mind of a Killer
- **Why:** Outside-in view catches blind spots self-scores miss.
- 3 min silent — 3-sentence memo as platform AI lead killing their product.
- 2–3 volunteers read one sentence each if time.

## Slide 19 — The Prototype Bet
- **Why:** "Fireworks" — theory lands when something exists; separates builders from deck-builders.
- 15 min — v0 / Cursor / Lovable; shareable link is the bar.
- Pair with someone who has an account if needed.

## Slide 20 — Synthesis
- **Why:** Lock learning into Component 1 so today isn't vibes.
- They fill Bet section: what / who / why now / archetype / scores / vulnerability / confidence.
- Tie discomfort from pair + killer memo → probabilistic world diagnosis.

## Slide 21 — Bridge to M2
- **Why:** Clear carry artifacts + hook ("can anyone copy this in 6 months?").
- Bring: scorecard, Component 1, prototype.
- M2 = data flywheel + vendor / copy risk — one sentence, don't teach M2 here.

## Slide 22 — Survey
- **Why:** Close loop for Product School; takes 60 sec.
- Thank them; QR or link — optional "one thing that surprised you" if you use a live form.
