# Speaker Notes: Module 1 — The Bet

Teaching notes for each slide. Not a script — key things to say, energy cues, and timing guidance.

---

## Slide 1 — Title (1 min)

> "Welcome. This is not a lecture. By the end of today you'll have diagnosed where your product strategy breaks under AI conditions, built a prototype with AI tools, and written the first section of a strategy brief you'll present to the room in Module 6. Let's go."

- Set the energy immediately. No preamble. No "let me tell you about myself" yet.
- The three waypoints (Diagnose, Discover, Decide) are the session's backbone. Point to them.

---

## Slide 2 — Agenda (1 min)

> "Quick flyover of the 2 hours. Heavy on applied work — three exercises, one case study, one prototype build. The teaching blocks are short. You'll spend most of today working on your own product."

- Don't read every agenda item. Hit the highlights: "Applied work is the longest block. The Prototype Bet at the end is your homework for M2."
- Move fast. They'll experience the flow, not memorize it.

---

## Slide 3 — Opening Question (2 min)

> "Show of hands. How many of you are adding AI to an existing product? And how many are building something AI-native from scratch?"

> "Great — we have both. This session works for both. The strategic questions are the same. What changes is the starting point, not the destination."

- Don't analyze the split. Just register it and move on.
- This replaces the old internal-framed question. The frame is product-first, not org-first.

---

## Slide 4 — Baseline Write (3 min)

> "Individual work. Silent. Your CEO walks into your office right now and asks: 'What's our AI strategy?' Write your answer. Three sentences. Don't overthink it."

> "Keep what you write. You'll revisit this in Module 6 and see how far you've traveled. This is Artifact #1."

- Give exactly 3 minutes. Timer visible if possible.
- Don't share. This is private baseline measurement.

---

## Slide 5 — True/False (8 min)

> "Three statements. Vote first — show of hands or Slack poll. Then I reveal. Then we argue."

Reveal sequence:
1. **"The best AI product wins" — FALSE.** "Copycats are everywhere. GPT wrappers with billion-dollar valuations are becoming margin-negative. If OpenAI ships your feature natively, product quality is irrelevant."

2. **"You can predict which AI feature will win" — FALSE.** "AI systems produce probability distributions, not guarantees. The skill is making bets under uncertainty, not pretending you have certainty."

3. **"Your discovery process is fast enough" — FALSE.** "Traditional discovery: 6–8 weeks. AI-powered discovery: days. If you're running quarterly research cycles, your competitors are running daily ones."

- The room should be uncomfortable by #3. That's the point.
- If nobody disagrees: "If none of these surprised you, you're either lying or in the wrong course."

---

## Slide 6 — The Arc (3 min)

> "Six strategic questions. Each builds on the last. Today: The Bet — what should we build, and how do we know fast?"

> "Each session adds a layer to one document: your AI Product Strategy Brief. By Module 6, you present the complete brief. The room plays your board."

- Point to each arc node. Don't explain M2–M6 in detail — just name them.
- Highlight the Strategy Brief: "Not worksheets. One living document. Board-ready by M6."

---

## Slide 7 — Annual Cycles Are Dead (3 min)

> "Cisco's CPO said it plainly: in AI, outcomes are inherently variable and unpredictable. The old planning model assumed you could commit to outcomes. AI broke that assumption."

> "The shift: from committing to outcomes — to making bets under irreducible uncertainty. From 'we will ship X' to 'we have 70% confidence this bet delivers Y, and here's our kill criteria.'"

- Paraphrase the quote, don't read it.
- Ask: "How many of you are still running annual planning cycles?" Pause. Let it sink in.

---

## Slide 8 — Discovery Is Too Slow (3 min)

> "Notion collapsed a 6-week research cycle into 4 days using AI-assisted insight clustering. Not by cutting corners — by using synthetic personas, automated synthesis, and segment simulation to get equivalent signal faster."

> "How long does your discovery cycle take? Be honest. 4 weeks? 8 weeks? What if you could get equivalent signal in 4 days?"

- The Notion example is your anchor. Make it concrete.
- "AI-powered discovery" includes: synthetic personas, automated insight synthesis, real-time segment simulation. Name all three.

---

## Slide 9 — Probabilistic Thinking (4 min)

> "Old way: 'We will ship X by Q3 and it will drive Y outcome.' New way: 'We have 70% confidence this bet delivers Y. Here's the validation plan and kill criteria.'"

> "Google's Bard-to-Gemini rebrand wasn't a marketing failure. It was an acknowledgment that probabilistic product bets require narrative pivots, not apologies."

- This is the conceptual anchor of M1. Take your time.
- The old/new comparison visual drives the point home. Point to it.

---

## Slide 10 — Case Study: Notion (7 min)

Walk through three acts:
- **The Bet:** Notion bet AI could transform their editor into a thinking tool.
- **The Crack:** Traditional research would have taken 6–8 weeks. Competitors would ship first.
- **The Correction:** AI-assisted clustering → 4-day research cycle. Shipped while competitors researched.

> "Discussion: How long does your discovery cycle take? What decisions are you making slowly that competitors are making in days?"

- 2–3 responses from the room. Then move on.

---

## Slide 11 — 5 AI Value Archetypes (8 min)

> "Not all AI bets have the same risk/return profile. Before you build, know which archetype you're building."

Walk through each row — 1 minute per archetype:
- **Automator:** Replaces labor. Klarna. High volume, margin pressure.
- **Copilot:** Augments humans. GitHub Copilot. Human stays in loop.
- **Oracle:** Predicts what humans can't see. Palantir. High WTP, high governance.
- **Creator:** Generates content. Jasper. Getting copied fast.
- **Orchestrator:** Multi-agent workflows. Highest complexity, highest governance.

> "Which archetype is your product? That answer shapes everything in Modules 2 through 5."

- This framework recurs throughout the course. Make sure they internalize the names.

---

## Slide 12 — Break (5 min)

> "5 minutes. When you come back: applied work for the rest of the session. Bring your laptop and your honesty."

---

## Slide 13 — Three-Axis Diagnostic (2 min)

> "Three axes. Contextual Moat: how deep are you in the workflow? Data Advantage: do you have data that compounds? Platform Exposure: what happens when Big Tech ships your feature?"

> "Score each 1–5. If you give yourself all 5s, you're lying. Your partner will call you on it in 15 minutes."

- Name the three axes explicitly. This is the framework they carry to M2.

---

## Slide 14 — Live Diagnosis (15 min)

> "Let's do this together first. [Pull up Notion/Figma/Linear/Slack.] Where is their contextual moat? What happens when Apple ships this natively? What's their one defensible advantage?"

- Let the room argue. The energy should be messy and real.
- Prep 2–3 fallback products. If nobody engages with the first choice, switch.
- This is the session's centerpiece by time. Don't rush it.

---

## Slide 15 — Your Diagnostic (10 min)

> "Now it's your turn. Score your own product. Three axes. Be ruthless. This scorecard travels with you — your M2 partner will attack it."

- Walk the room. Check people are scoring numerically, not just thinking.
- Prompt the stuck: "Start with Platform Exposure. Who's the biggest threat?"

---

## Slide 16 — Pair Challenge (10 min)

> "5 minutes per person. Your partner presents their diagnostic. Your job: challenge the score, not validate it. You MUST identify at least one axis where the score should be lower. Then name one AI-native competitor attacking that vulnerability. Not hypothetically — name the company."

- Give 60 seconds of solo thinking before the naming.
- This is the first real peer pressure moment. It's supposed to be uncomfortable.

---

## Slide 17 — In the Mind of a Killer (3 min)

> "Silent writing. You're the Head of AI at the platform whose job is to destroy your product. 3-sentence memo to your CEO: what you attack, what your wedge is, why users switch."

> "The blind spots you find here are the ones self-assessment always misses."

- 2–3 volunteers share theirs. Quick reactions from the room.

---

## Slide 18 — The Prototype Bet (15 min)

> "You've diagnosed where your strategy breaks. Now build something. Take one idea from today and make it tangible with v0, Cursor, or Lovable. Not polished. Not production. Just a shareable link."

> "This exercise immediately separates leaders who understand the prototype-first era from those still theorizing about it."

- Pre-work: tool accounts should be set up. If not, pair with someone who has one.
- Minimum output: a link.
- Walk the room. Help people who are stuck on tool mechanics.

---

## Slide 19 — Synthesis (5 min)

> "Every vulnerability you found, every discomfort from the pair challenge, every 'oh no' in the killer memo — those exist because you've been planning deterministically in a probabilistic world."

> "Today was the diagnosis. Modules 2 through 6 are the reconstruction. Fill in Section 1 of your Strategy Brief now."

- Name the Strategy Brief fields: what you're building, for whom, why now, archetype, three-axis scores, biggest vulnerability, confidence level.
- This is the moment the module's thesis crystallizes. Don't rush it.

---

## Slide 20 — Bridge to M2 (2 min)

> "You know what you're betting on. M2 answers: can anyone copy this in 6 months? Bring your diagnostic scorecard, your Strategy Brief Section 1, and your prototype. M2 starts where you left off."

- Name what they're bringing. Make the continuity concrete.
- End with energy: "Today you diagnosed. Starting next session, you rebuild."

---

## General Teaching Tips

- **The room is the asset.** These are senior PMs. They know things you don't. Let them argue in the live diagnosis and pair work. Your job is to channel the energy, not control it.
- **Timing is tight.** If the live diagnosis runs long (common), cut 2–3 minutes from the prototype bet. The prototype is important but the diagnostic is the session's core.
- **Don't explain the exercises.** Show the slide. Read the instruction. Start the timer. Explanation kills momentum with senior audiences.
- **The killer memo is the emotional peak.** Give it space. The room gets quiet during writing. That's good.
- **External focus always.** Every question is about their product, not their team. If someone asks about internal org changes, redirect: "Great question — M5 covers org topology. Today we're focused on the product bet."
