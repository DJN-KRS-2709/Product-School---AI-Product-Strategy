## Slide 4 — Provocation
- "Having the most data wins" — nope, data volume is a commodity. What compounds is proprietary data — corrections, preferences, feedback loops only your product generates
- "Better product = users won't switch" — nope, brand is not a moat anymore. Gokul explicitly excluded it from his 8 Moats. Switching costs heading to zero. Only workflow depth prevents switching
- "We can always switch providers" — nope, most products are hard-wired to one model. No abstraction layer = hostage
- Ask: "who could swap their AI provider in 48 hours?" — almost nobody

## Slide 5 — Systems of Intelligence vs. Systems of Record
- System of Record stores stuff (a CRM with call logs). Anyone can build one
- System of Intelligence learns from stuff — corrections, preferences, domain feedback
- The moat is the gap between what a foundation model knows and what your system has learned from your users

## Slide 5b — The 8 Moats Landscape
- Gokul's framework: Data, Workflow, Regulatory, Distribution, Ecosystem, Network, Physical, Scale
- 4+ total = secure, 2-3 = weak, 0-1 = in trouble
- Quick example: Atlassian scores ~3 (data, workflow, ecosystem), Monday scores ~1 (light workflow). Both down 75% — but Atlassian is probably oversold, Monday might be rightly priced
- For AI builders, Data and Workflow are the two that really matter

## Slide 6 — The Data Flywheel
- Three types of data that compound: corrections, preferences, domain context
- The cycle: interact → capture → improve → better outputs → more interaction
- Key question: does your product capture this stuff or throw it away?

## Slide 7 — Contextual Moats
- Horizontal AI = bolted on, easy to copy. Vertical AI = embedded so deep that leaving means rebuilding the process
- GitHub Copilot example — it's not just autocomplete, it's in the editor, PRs, review, CI. Leaving means changing how the team writes software
- Workflow depth is a spectrum: NetSuite runs your business (deep moat) vs. Zendesk handles tickets (light moat)

## Slide 8 — Cold-Start Survival
- Four strategies when you have no data: synthetic seeding, transfer learning, partnership data, human-in-the-loop bootstrapping
- Cold-start isn't fatal, it's temporary — the question is whether you're designed to escape it

## Slide 9 — Case Study: Jasper vs. ChatGPT
- Bet: $125M raised, $1.5B valuation, 70K customers, fastest AI startup 2022
- Crack: ChatGPT launched, then OpenAI added plugins, Google shipped Gemini into Workspace. AI writing became free and native
- Correction: pivoted to enterprise marketing workflows. Gokul said Jasper went "from 1 to 100 and 100 to 40." Bolt-on AI doesn't change your defensibility score
- Ask the room: where is your product a wrapper vs. a workflow?

## Slide 10 — Data Flywheel Scoring
- Four loops: Correction, Preference, Domain Context, Network
- Score each 1-5. Weakest loop = where competitors attack first

## Slide 13 — Encroachment Threat Assessment

## Slide 14 — Peer Challenge: Big Tech Attacker
- 6 min per person
- Defender presents flywheel + threats. Attacker gives a specific 90-day plan to kill the product
- Has to name the weakest flywheel loop and exploit it

## Slide 15 — The Kill Switch
- Three layers: abstraction layer (no direct provider calls), multi-model routing (tasks go to different models by cost/quality), eval harness (automated quality check before switching)
- Gokul's point: switching costs are heading to zero, agents can move data across tools, UX can be cloned
- If you can't swap providers in 48 hours, you have a dependency, not a strategy

## Slide 16 — Your Portability Audit
- 15 min build moment — audit vendor dependency
- Two stress tests: provider doubles pricing, provider restricts your use case
- Walk out with three action items: this week, this month, this quarter
