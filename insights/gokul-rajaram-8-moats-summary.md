# Insight Source: Gokul Rajaram — The 8 Moats Companies Need

**Source:** 20VC with Harry Stebbings, Episode 239 (March 16, 2026)
**Guest:** Gokul Rajaram — Board Director at Coinbase, Pinterest, The Trade Desk. Ex-DoorDash, Square, Facebook, Google. 700+ angel investments including Figma (500-1000x), Airtable, Vercel.

---

## The 8 Moats Framework

Gokul's play on Hamilton Helmer's Seven Powers — adapted for the AI era. Score a company across all eight. Anything 4+ is "pretty damn secure." 2-3 is weak. 0-1 means you need to build more or you're screwed.

| # | Moat | Definition | Example |
|---|------|-----------|---------|
| 1 | **Data** | Truly proprietary data nobody else has access to. Must get better with time. | Spotify's decade of listening behavior powering Discover |
| 2 | **Workflow** | How deeply embedded in the company's operations. Weak alone, but depth matters. | NetSuite (deep, runs the business) vs. Zendesk (light, replaceable) |
| 3 | **Regulatory** | Licenses, capital requirements, multi-year procurement contracts. | Coinbase's money transmission licenses, state by state |
| 4 | **Distribution** | Proprietary or exclusive distribution channels. | Intuit trained an entire network of CPAs to use only QuickBooks |
| 5 | **Ecosystem** | Third-party platform where many developers/partners have built and rely on you. | Shopify: 100K+ developers, every merchant uses 5-6 third-party apps |
| 6 | **Network** | Marketplace density, reputation history, network effects. AI can't vibe-code liquidity. | DoorDash: restaurant density, delivery speed, reputation |
| 7 | **Physical Infrastructure** | Atoms are hard to displace. | Humanoid robots are years away from replacing physical moats |
| 8 | **Scale** | Cost advantage through scale so low it's hard to replicate. | Amazon, TSMC. Pure software companies can't claim this anymore. |

### Scoring Examples

| Company | Score | Moats Present |
|---------|-------|---------------|
| Atlassian | ~3 | Data (code checked in), Workflow (deep), Ecosystem (third-party apps) |
| Monday | ~1 | Workflow (light) |

## Key Insights Mapped to Course Modules

### M1: The Bet

- **Non-consumption markets are the biggest hits.** Shopify didn't target existing e-commerce merchants — it enabled anyone to sell. Granola created standalone AI note-taking nobody knew they needed. Gamma took slides out of Google's suite. The biggest bets create new behaviors, not better versions of old ones.
- **Pattern matching kills great bets.** Gokul passed on Quinc (D2C, "category is dead") — it raised at $10B. Sequoia's Mike Moritz invested in Instacart after losing $370M on Webvan. First principles > pattern matching.
- **Remarkable product at the core.** From Google: "If there is not a remarkable product, all the go-to-market and distribution in the world will not save you." The product must be 10x-100x better than the alternative (Gmail offering 1GB when Yahoo offered 10MB).

### M2: The Moat ← PRIMARY MODULE

- **The 8 Moats scoring system** is a more comprehensive version of our Three-Axis Diagnostic. Consider incorporating it as the advanced framework — or at minimum, referencing it alongside our Data/Workflow/Platform axes.
- **"Data and workflow moats are the two things you're really hanging your hat on as a software investor."** For pure software companies, these are the only two early-stage moats that matter. Ecosystem, distribution, network are too early to assess. Physical and scale don't apply.
- **Data moat requires compounding.** "Go deep into what is the data asset you're creating. Does it get better with time? Are you fine-tuning a model and improving it over time?" — This directly validates our Data Flywheel framework.
- **Workflow depth is a spectrum.** NetSuite (runs the business, score 1.0) vs. Zendesk (light workflow, score 0.5). The question isn't whether you have workflow embedding — it's how deep. This nuance strengthens our Contextual Moat axis.
- **Brand is NOT a moat anymore.** Gokul explicitly excluded it from his framework. Reason: switching costs going to essentially zero. Data portability becoming easy. Ability to clone UX pixel by pixel. "In that case, what is that brand really?"
- **Switching costs going to zero.** Agents will make data migration from systems of record increasingly easy (Seb from Cloner cited). This accelerates the encroachment threat in our framework.
- **Bolt-on AI has a ceiling.** Companies that just add AI features don't change the defensibility equation. The ones that work "reframe what the product does, not just add capabilities." Must "rebuild the entire experience end to end." Notion is an example doing it right — but "the AI agents need to now get better based on how the user interacts with it and they need to tune the model for their customer base. Most bolt-on players are not doing it."
- **Jasper cited as cautionary tale.** "They went from 1 to 40 and then came back from 40 to 10 or 1 to 100 and 100 to 40 within very quick time frames." Validates our case study choice for M2.

### M3: The Margin

- **Seat pricing vs. outcome-based pricing.** Two kinds of products: access products (seat-based, e.g. ChatGPT Enterprise) and work products (outcome-based, e.g. Harvey charges per contract processed). "Seat pricing breaks when the product's core value is not about access but about something doing the work on your behalf."
- **Margins don't matter in year 1-2.** "I don't look at margins. Durability and defensibility is much more of a worry. Good companies, if they're defensible, will have the ability to increase margins." But the *potential* to increase prices matters — PayPal raised prices 5 times in 3 years because of stickiness.
- **Margin improvement: price increases > cost decreases.** "I would rather see margins go up with price increases than cost decreases." Implies defensibility → pricing power → margins. Validates the M2→M3 sequence in our arc.
- **Inference costs are dropping, margins will improve.** But the key question is whether the company has the defensibility/leverage to also increase prices. Otherwise, cost drops just get competed away.
- **Commoditize the complement.** Salesforce's strategic choice: is the profit pool in data or workflows? If workflows, make data storage free and switch to outcome-based pricing. If data, give away agentic workflows and charge for data. "They need to commoditize a complement. They can't just wait around."
- **Multi-product portfolio for margin and retention.** Square went from 1 to 11 products, each $50M+ revenue. Key insight: "Not every product needs to generate profit. Some are for money, some are for retention. If you confuse the two, your teams are built for the wrong outcomes."
- **Scale moat is dead for pure software.** "Earlier, software was a scale game where because you had produced a lot of software it was cheaper to produce more. Now everybody can produce software as cheaply as anybody else." Only hyperscalers and physical companies still have scale moats.

### M5: The Guardrails

- **Vertical AI must own the full stack.** Can't be one function in a vertical. Service Titan: 32 products, still only ~$10B. Vertical products must go deep across the entire workflow to build defensibility.
- **Model capabilities change every 6 months.** "You can't have too long a product road map because your road map is going to be blown out by the next model iteration." Validates our continuous-sprint operating model from M1.
- **AI is shifting from SaaS to services.** "It's no longer SaaS — it's services. You're going after the services spend." The labor budget is the new TAM, not the software budget.

### M6: The Pitch

- **Revenue durability > growth rate.** "It's not about margins, it's about is this revenue durable? Retention is the fundamental indicator of quality." Would always take triple-triple-double-double with excellent retention over 10x growth with bad retention.
- **Investors score moats.** The 8-moat scoring system is how smart investors evaluate defensibility. Teaching participants to score their own product helps them speak the language their board and investors use.

5. **M3 "Commoditize the Complement" Concept:** Salesforce's choice (profit pool in data vs. workflows) is an excellent M3 teaching moment about where the margin lives.

6. **M2 Kill Switch:** "Switching costs going to zero" + data portability via agents validates the entire Kill Switch exercise. The world is moving toward easier switching — companies that haven't built the abstraction layer are even more exposed than they think.
